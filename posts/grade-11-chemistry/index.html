Mixtures & Pure Substances

  • Pure substances cannot be broken down by physical means - only chemical
  • Substances contain 2+ substances not joined together by chemical means
  • Mixtures
    • Homogenous (aka solution)
      • Uniform appearance
    • Heterogeneous
      • Distinctly different substances can be seen (phases)

Pure substances

  • Element
    • Atoms
    • Molecules
      • 2+ atoms bonded together
  • Compound
    • Molecules
      • 2+ different atoms bonded together

Nomenclature

Ionic compounds:
  • Two elements: metal name + non-metal root + “ide”
    • E.g., NaCl = Sodium Chloride
  • w/ Transition metal ions: metal(charge) + non-metal root + “ide”
    • E.g, Cu(OH)2 =Copper(II) Hydroxide
  • w/ Polyatomic ion: metal name + polyatomic ion
    • E.g, Al2(SO4)3 = Aluminum Sulfate
Molecular compounds
  • Prefixes are used to count the number of atoms
  • Add “ide” on the 2nd element
    • E.g, H2O2 = Dihydrogen dioxide
Diatomic Molecules
  • Some atoms cannot naturally exist by itself (too unstable), and thus will bond with each other
  • Br I N Cl H O F

Percentage Composition

  • When chemists analyze unknown compound and need to determine structure of a chemical one
    • One useful analysis technique involves determining the amount of mass of each type of atom in the unknown
    • AKA, percentage composition (provides relative percent of each atom in the sample)
      • Tells us the amount of element present in the compound
  • Sample mass are measured with ~8 decimal places for % composition analysis in a lab
    • +/- microgram accuracy

%Composition Example

  • A 5 g sample contains the elements Ca and Cl, if the mass of Cl in the sample is 3.19 g, what is the percent composition of Ca and Cl in the sample?

$$\%Cl = \frac{3.19}{5.000} 100\%$$ $$= 63.8\%$$ $$\%Ca = 100\% - 63.8\%$$ $$=36.2\%$$

  • A 7.53 g sample contains Li and Cl, if the mass of Li in the sample is 1.233 g, what is the percent composition of Li and Cl in the sample?

$$\%Li = \frac{1.233}{7.53} 100\%$$ $$=16.4\%$$ $$\%Cl = 100\% - 16.4\%$$ $$= 83.6\%$$

%Composition with a Chemical Formula

  • Previous example, masses of each atom was known
  • We can also use the chemical formula to determine the % composition using periodic table and atomic mass (in amu), of each element in the compound

Practice %Composition with a Chemical Formula

  • Step 1: Calc mass of each element and add them up to find total mass of compound
  • Step 2: Divide mass of all the atoms of each element by total mass of compound, to %
  • What is the % composition by mass of the elements in Al2(SO4)3?

Isotopes

  • Atoms of the same element may naturally be found with different atomic mass due to varying amount of neutrons
  • Atomic mass of each element in the periodic table is the weighted average of isotopes for that element
  • E.g., Carbon-12, Carbon-13, Carbon 14
  • Weighted avg can be calculated using
    • mass1 x weight percent / 100 + mass2 x weight percent / 100 +..,+etc
  • Percent abundance = weight factor of each isotope or the percentage that the isotope makes up out of all other isotopes
  • Avg atomic mass = weighted avg of isotopes of that element
  • Isotopic abundance = abundance of an isotope of an element found naturally on earth

History of Atom/Atomic Theory

Atomos - greek for indivisible

  • Basic unit of an element

400BC - Democritus

  • Came up with idea of indivisible particles - atomos
  • Idea was rejected by philosophers (e.g., Aristotle)

350BC - Aristotle

  • Earth, Fire, Water, Air

1808 - John Dalton

  • Brought back atomos idea + 4 rules of matter
    • All matter is made of atoms
    • Each element has its own kind of atom
    • Compounds are created when you mix elements in definite proportions
    • Atoms cannot be created or destroyed

1897 - JJ Thomson

  • Plum pudding model w/ electrons inside and positive mass as dough
    • Used cathode ray tubes to show that atoms contain negatively charged subatomic particles (tube ended up being negatively charged)
    • Since atoms are neutral overall, he reasoned there must be positive charge to counteract the negative charges
      • Led to plum pudding proposal

1911 - Ernest Rutherford

  • Discovers nucleus and that atom is mostly empty space

  • Gold foil experiment - discovered atom is mostly empty space w/ nucleus at center and electrons orbiting

    • Charged alpha particles are aimed at gold foil with the prediction that most will pass straight through with minimal deflection (since mass and charge were thought to be uniformly distributed)
    • In actuality, while most did pass through, there were extreme deflections

1913 - Neils Bohr

  • Planetary model of atom
  • Energy levels
    • Used to explain different line spectra of elements
      • e- starts at ground state
      • e- is capable of moving from one orbital to another if electricity is added (excited state), gives off energy like light

1932 - Heisenberg/Chadwick

  • Led to quantum model of atom + electron clouds
  • Showed that it is impossible to find exact pos of e- or momentum and vice versa (Heisenberg uncertainty principle)
  • Showed that e- behaved like waves

Mass spectrometer

  • Instrument used to study structures of elements and compounds
    • Can be used to determine precisely the mass and abundance of isotopes
  • Measures mass to charge ratio of molecules present in a sample
  • Can be used to calculate exact molecular weight of the sample
  • Mass spectrometer has
    • Ionization source
      • Turn molecules into ions (usually negatively charged by removing an e-)
    • Mass analyzer
      • After ionization, ions are sorted and separated according to mass to charge ratios by passing them through an electric and then magnetic field
    • Ion detection system
      • Computerized detector tallies ions in diff parts of the spectrum and figure out what kind of atoms were present in sample (mass to charge ratio and relative abundance are stored)

Atomic Theory - Quantum Model

Introduction

  • Bohr model of atom / planetary model
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus in specific energy levels

Light & EM Spectrum

  • Light
    • Travels fast 3.00 x 10^8 m/s in a vacuum
    • Travels in a straight line
    • Light is an EM wave
    • EM waves do not need particles to travel
  • EM Spectrum
    • RMIVUXG (Robert’s Mother Is Visiting Uncle Xavier’s Garden)
    • EM spectrum comprises of different types of waves
    • Different waves have different wavelengths and frequencies
    • Min/max = crests and troughs
    • Distance between crests = wavelength (period)
    • Frequency = # of cycles that pass a pt in a second (Hz)

Atomic Spectra

  • Each atom starts at a ground state
  • When atoms absorb energy, e- move to an excited state
    • E- then lose energy to return to ground state (e.g., in form of light)
  • Visible light = 400-700nm
  • Each element has its own unique characteristic and emit different colors in a cathode ray tube/discharge tube
    • Corresponds to different results in line spectra

Problem w/ Bohr Model

  • Does explain spectral line of Hydrogen where there is different colors
  • Does not explain multiple lines of same colors
  • Scientists needed a new model to explain this (quantum model)

Quantum Model

  • Each energy level has a sub energy level
    • e- does not move in precise orbits
    • Energy level represented by n
      • Can only contain max # of e- of 2n^2 in each energy level
      • Periods on periodic table also reps energy level
  • e- moves in precise regions of space called orbitals
    • e- in each energy level are confined to specific regions
    • Orbitals = areas most likely to find e-

  • Energy levels have overlapping subshells divided into 4 different types
    • Defined by regions in periodic table - s,p,d,f

Principles for Filling Electrons

Aufbau Principle
  • Start filling orbitals from lowest to highest energy

Hund’s Rule
  • No pairing of electrons in any orbital until each orbital at that level has one e-
Pauli’s Exclusion Principle
  • No orbital may contain more than 2 e- because no 2 e- can have the same 4 quantum numbers

Quantum Numbers

  • Each e- can be described by four quantum numbers (like an address)

  1. First/Principle Quantum Number, n

    • Represents energy level

  2. Second Quantum Number, l (angular momentum)

    • l = 0, 1, 2 … (n-1)
    • Determines shape of orbital(s,p,d,f)
      • l = 0, s orbital
      • l = 1, p orbital
      • l = 2, d orbital
      • l = 3, f orbital

  3. Third Quantum Number, ml (magnetic quantum number)

    • ml = -l to +l
    • Determines number of orbitals in subshell and its orientation

  4. Fourth Quantum Number, ms (spin quantum number)

    • Determines electron orientation
    • ms = -½ (down arrow) or +½ (up arrow)

Unit 2 - Bonding and Reactions

Ionic and Covalent Bonds + Nomenclature

Ionic Bonds

  • e- transferred completely
  • Bonds between metal and nonmetal (e.g., MgO)
    • Since metals tend to lose valence electrons to become stable, they become positive ions (cations), and transfer electrons to a nonmetal with a stronger attraction.
  • Ionic bonds are very polar (more than covalent bonds)
  • Drawing Binary Ionic Compounds
    • Draw LDD
    • Use arrows to show e- mvmt
    • Write final compounds and use subscripts to show # of atoms
    • E.g., Al + O –> Al2O3
    • Criss cross method:
      • Write charges/bonding capacity to become stable
      • Al3 + O2- –> Al2O3 (reduce subscripts when possible)
  • Since ionic bonds form in one way, naming is simple
    • Metal name + nonmetal name + ide
  • Transition metals (w/ multivalent electrons)
    • CuO –> reverse criss cross –> Copper (II) Oxide
    • Cu2O –> Copper (I) Oxide
  • Polyatomic ions
    • E.g., sulfate (SO4)2-
    • Naming: Metal + polyatomic ion

Covalent Bonds

  • e- are shared
  • Bonds between 2 nonmetals (e.g., CH4)
    • Since nonmetals tend to hold electrons tightly, they are more likely to share electrons rather than losing electrons
  • Drawing Binary Covalent Compounds
    • Calculate # of valence electrons
    • Fill bonds, 2e- each
    • Fill central atom w/ octet rule
    • Double check total e-
      • E.g., CH4 (methane)
      • C - 4x1, H - 1x4, total e- = 8
  • Covalent have a max 3 bonds (usually) b/w covalent atoms (max bonding capacity)
  • Naming
    • Name central atom (if more than one, add prefix)
    • Name bonded atom w/ prefix for # of atoms bonded
    • CH4 => Carbon tetrahydride
    • CO2 => Carbon dioxide
  • Polarity
    • Separation of charges
  • Polar covalent bond
    • A bond where two atoms have different electronegativities, causing separation of charges
      • Electron of one atom may be more attracted to the other, causing one atom to be slightly negative and the other slightly positive positive
  • Nonpolar covalent bond
    • A bond between two atoms that are the same, causing equal distribution of electrons
    • A bond between two atoms with very similar electronegativities, causing an almost equal distribution of electrons

Molecular Shapes

  • Electron Group Arrangement - defined by both bonding and nonbonding electron groups
  • Molecular shape: 3d arrangement of nuclei joined by bonding groups - defined only by relative positions of nuclei

  • Bond angle is angle formed by nuclei of two surrounding atoms with the nucleus of the central atom

  • Often, real bond angles deviate from ideal bond angles

VSEPR Theory - Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion

  • Electrons pairs repel other electron pairs, which make molecules take on certain shapes
  • The number of “electron concentration areas” around the central atom determines the molecule’s shape

Factors affecting bond angles

  • Nonbonding/lone pairs (around central atom)

    • Lone pairs repels bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other, and decreasing angle between bonding pairs

    • Double bonds has greater electron density than a single bond and repels single bond electrons more than single bond pairs repel each other

Molecular Shapes w/ 5+ electron groups

  • Five electron group sys has 2 diff pos for electron groups and two ideal bond angles
  • When possible, lone pairs occupy equatorial positions

Shapes

  • Linear - 2 electron concentration area around central atom
  • Trigonal Planar - 3 electron concentration area around central atom
  • Tetrahedral - 4 electron concentration area around central atom
    • Trigonal Pyramidal - Has one lone pair at the top
  • Bent
    • Lone pairs around central atom will “bend” bonds away (they repel bonds more than bonds repel each other)
    • Double bonds have a greater electron density than single bonds and will repel single bonds more than single bonds repel each other

Polyatomic Ions + Oxoacids

  • Nick the Camel ate Clam for Supper in Phoenix
    • Vowels = charge (-)
    • Consonants = # of oxygens
    • Bolded = element
      • Nick: NO3- Nitrate
      • Camel: CO3 2- Carbonate
      • Clam: ClO3- Chlorate

  • Hydrogen ions (H+) can join with above polyatomic ions and become acids
    • Hydrogen + Nitrate –> H3NO Nitric acid (industry use, fertilizers)
    • Hydrogen + Carbonate –> H2CO3 Carbonic acid (carbonated beverages)

    • Hydrogen + Phosphate –> H3PO4 Phosphoric acid (industry use, fertilizer)

Determining Bond Type

  • Electronegativity of elements can help determine types of bond that will be made (ionic/covalent)
    • If change in electronegativity b/w two elements < 0.4 - nonpolar covalent bond e.g., (F2)
    • If change in electronegativity > 0.4 and < 1.7, polar covalent bond e.g., (NF3)
    • If change in electronegativity > 1.7, ionic bond (e.g., NaF)

Intramolecular and Intermolecular Forces

Intramolecular Force

  • Forces within a molecule (i.e., bonds)

Intermolecular Force

  • Forces between (covalent) molecules (weakest to strongest)
    • London Dispersion Force
      • Weakest force - occurs with all molecules, including non polar molecules
      • At times, electron distribution may change slightly, causing one side of a molecule to be slightly positive and the other being slightly negative (temporary dipoles)
        • This causes a weak attraction between two molecules
    • Dipole Dipole Force
      • The attraction between two (permanent) dipoles (molecule with opposite charges / polar molecule)
        • Dipoles: a bond/molecule whose ends have opposite charges
    • Hydrogen Bonds
      • Very electronegative and a special type of dipole dipole attraction
      • Like a “fake” electrostatic force
        • Only with F, O, N elements
    • Electrostatic Force
      • Ionic compounds

Solubility of polar covalent and nonpolar covalent compounds

  • Like “dissolves” like

    • Polar compounds dissolve in polar compounds (e.g., H2O)

      • Dipole dipole attraction allows compounds to attract one another due to molecular geometry

      • (2) Do polar molecules dissolve in water? Why or why not? - Quora

    • Non polar molecules dissolve in nonpolar solvents due to london dispersion forces weakly attracting each other

      • Non polar molecules can become weak dipoles and briefly attract other molecules (LDF)
      • London Dispersion Interactions - Chemistry LibreTexts

Melting points of polar/nonpolar, and ionic compounds

  • Ionic compounds are attracted by electrostatic attraction (ionic bonds)
    • Since electrostatic attraction is the strongest attraction force, a lot of energy is required to break the bond
    • Thus, ionic bonds are hard to break
  • Polar compounds are attracted by a dipole dipole force
    • Weaker than electrostatic forces, requiring less energy to break the bond
    • Thus, polar covalent bonds are slightly easy to break
  • Nonpolar compounds are attracted by a LDF (weakest attraction force), requiring the least energy to break bonds
    • Thus, nonpolar bonds are easy to break

Dipole Moment

  • Net polarity of a molecule
  • Can be thought of as a resultant vector of a vector addition of all covalent polar bonds

Bond Polarity and Molecule Polarity

  • EN - ability of atom to attract shared electrons
  • Electrons spend most of their time around atoms w/ greater EN

Nonpolar Covalent Bond

  • When electrons are shared b/w 2 atoms, covalent bond is formed
  • If atoms are identical or have nearly the same EN values, (e.g., Cl2), electrons are shared equally (nonpolar)

Polar Covalent Bond

  • If electrons are shared between 2 different atoms (e.g, HBr), sharing is unequal
  • Bonding electrons spend more time near more electronegative atom
    • This type of charge separation, bond is a polar covalent bond

Polar Molecules

  • Not all molecules with polar bonds are polar molecules

Determine Polarity of Molecules:

  • Polarity of a molecule depends on polarity of bonds and shape of molecule

To determine polarity of molecule

  • Determine if there are polar bonds using change in EN value
  • If there are polar bonds, establish direction of dipoles

  • Identify shape of molecule
    • If dipoles cancel due to symmetrical shape of molecule, then compound is nonpolar
      • (i.e., linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral)
    • If dipoles do not cancel out then compound is polar
      • Asymmetrical/bent molecule (lone pairs)

Intramolecular and Intermolecular Forces

Intramolecular Forces

  • Attractive forces between atoms & ions within a molecule
  • I.e., Covalent bonds (polar, nonpolar, double, triple, etc) and ionic bonds
  • Fairly strong

Intermolecular Forces

  • Attractive forces between molecules (weaker than intramolecular forces)
  • I.e., less energy required to melt H2O than decomposing H2 or O2
  • Strength of intermolecular forces determines
    • State
    • Melting/boiling pt
    • Surface tension
    • Hardness/texture
    • Solubility
    • Viscosity
Types of Intermolecular Forces
  • Van der Waals Forces

    • London Dispersion Force (LDF)

      • Weakest force
      • Occurs between all molecules (including nonpolar)
      • Results from temp displacements of e- around atoms in a molecule
        • Causes them to be short lived dipoles

    • Dipole-Dipole Force

      • Forces of attraction b/w oppositely charged ends of polar molecules
      • Fairly strong attractive force

  • Hydrogen Bonds
    • Very strong and electronegative (>1.7)
    • Almost like a fake ionic bond
    • Formed by (+) hydrogen atom and highly electronegative atom of the other molecule (F, O, N)

Unit 3 - Stoichiometry

  • Study of relationships b/w quantities of reactants and products involved in chemical rxns

  • Mole - SI unit for measuring # of entities (atoms/molecules) in a given sample (n or mol)

  • Avogadro’s number (a constant) $$ \text{The number of entities in 1 mole} = 6.022 \times 10^{23} $$ $$ 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ represents the number of atoms in 12g of C-12}$$

    • The number of atoms in 1 mol is the same for each element (Avogrado’s constant), but the mass of 1 mol of each element is different (depends on atomic mass)
      • Avogadro’s number is chosen such that the magnitude of the mass of one mole of a compound is equal to the average mass of one molecule of the compound in amu
        • Note that 1 amu depends on the mass of 1/12 of a C-12 atom
    • The mole helps express and measure a bunch of tiny things like atoms/compounds in grams

  • Molar mass - the mass (g) of 1 mole of element

    • Molar mass = magnitude of relative atomic mass in g with respect to 1 mole
    • E.g., molar mass of carbon is 12.01 g/mol

  • Molar mass of K2CO3

    • 6.02 x 10^23 molecules of K2CO3 in 1 mol
      • K: 2 x 6.02x10^23 atoms of potassium in 1 mol of the compound
      • C: 1 x 6.02x10^23 atoms of carbon in 1 mol of compound
      • O: 3 x 6.02x10^23 atoms of oxygen in 1 mole of compound
        • K: 2 x 39.10 g/mol
        • C: 12.01 g / mol
        • O: 3 x 16.00 g/mol
          • Total molar mass of K2CO3 is 138.21 g/mol

Example Questions

  • How many molecules in 3 moles of oxygen? $$ \text{The number of molecules} = 3\text{mol} \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{molecules/mol} $$ $$ = 1.8066 \times 10^{23} \text{molecules} $$

  • How many moles in 4.5 x 10^23 molecules of ammonia? $$ \text{The number of moles} = 4.5 \times 10^{23} \text{molecules} \times \frac{1\text{mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{molecules}} $$ $$ = 7.5 \text{mol} $$

  • How many g of water to make 6 moles? $$ \text{Molar mass of H2O} = 2 \times 1.01 \text{g/mol} + 16.00 \text{g/mol} = 18.02 \text{g/mol} $$ $$ \text{The number of grams} = 6 \text{mol} \times 18.02\text{g/mol} $$ $$ = 108.12\text{g of water} $$

  • How many moles in 80g of chlorine? $$ \text{Molar mass of Cl2} = 2 \times 35.45\text{g/mol} = 70.9\text{g/mol} $$ $$ \text{The number of moles} = 80\text{g} \times \frac{1\text{mol}}{70.9\text{g}} $$ $$ = 1.128 \text{moles of chlorine in 80g} $$

Limiting Reagent

  • Reagent = reactant
  • Limiting reagent = reagent that gets used up/runs out first in a chemical reaction rxn
    • Limits the amount of product that is produced
    • The reagent that produces the least product
    • Limiting Reagents - Chemistry LibreTexts - example/problem walkthrough

Excess Reagent

  • Try to produce products we want and minimizing products we don’t want in chem rxn
  • Often, slightly more reactant is added called the excess reagent
    • Chosen because it is cheaper
    • Or safety consideration if isn’t added

Percentage yield

  • After identifying limiting reagent, calculate the mass of product formed by using limiting reagent amount of moles
    • Maximum products that can be made (in mass) = theoretical yield
    • The products recovered after performing the rxn can be used to calculate % yield $$ \text{Percent Yield} = \frac{\text{Mass of products recovered (actual yield)}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} $$

Arrhenius Definition

  • Acids are substances that produce hydronium ions (H3O+) in aqueous solutions

    • Substance dissociates in water
    • Acids increase concentration of of hydrogen ions in aqueous solution
    • E.g., HCl(s) + H2O → H3O+(aq) + Cl-(aq)
      • Simplified: HCl(aq) → H+(aq) + Cl-(aq)

  • Bases are substances that produce hydroxide ions (OH-) in aqueous solutions

    • Substance dissociates in water
    • Bases increase concentration of hydroxide ions in aqueous solution
    • E.g., NaOH (aq) → Na+(aq) + OH-(aq) \

  • The definition of acidity and alkalinity is restricted to aq solutions

Bronsted-Lowry Definition (deals w/ protons)

  • Acids are proton donors (donates H+/H3O+)
    • E.g., HCL(aq) → H+(aq) + Cl-(aq)
  • Bases are proton acceptors (accepts and bonds to H+/H3O+)
    • E.g., OH-(aq) + H+(aq) → HOH(l)
  • The definition expanded on Arrhenius definition to include bases of nitrogen containing compounds
    • E.g., NH3(aq) + H+(aq) ⇌ NH4+(aq)
    • OR NH3(aq) + H2O(l) ⇌ NH4+(aq) + OH-(aq)
      • NH3 (Ammonia) is a weak base
      • Doesn’t contain hydroxide ions but reacts with water to produce ammonium ions and hydroxide ions

Lewis Definition (deals with e-)

  • Acids are e- pair acceptors
  • Bases are e- pair donors

  • E.g.,
    Lewis Acid Lewis Base

$$ H_\text{Lewis Acid} + Cl_\text{Lewis Base} \rightarrow HCl $$

  • Definition included everything under Arrhenius + Bronsted-Lowry definition, and expanded to include compounds not previously imagined by the two definitions

Measuring Acids + Bases

  • pH scale (power of hydrogen)

    • Measure strength of acids/bases by measuring concentration of H+ ions in solution

      • The further from pH 7, the stronger the acid/base

Calculating pH

  • Calculating pH $$ \text{Let [H+] represent the molar concentration of hydrogen ions present in mol/L} $$ $$ pH = -log_{10}([H+]) $$

  • Calculating concentration of H+ ions $$ [H+] = 10^{-pH} $$

Unit 5 - Gases

States of Matter

Arranged from less to more energy, and greatest to least intermolecular forces:

  • E.g., solid has less energy than a gas due to kinetic energy of atoms
  • E.g., gas has less intermolecular forces due to greater distance of atoms than a solid
  1. Bose Einstein condensate
    1. (atoms cooled to near absolute zero, and they start behaving as a one giant atom)
  2. Solid 2. Particles are close together and organized
  3. Liquid 3. Particles are close together but disorganized (particles are free to move around)
  4. Gas 4. Particles are spread far apart and disorganized
  5. Plasma (superheated matter such that electrons “fly” off) 5. Particles consisting of neutral atoms, ions, and e-, are far apart and disorganized 6. Affected by magnetic fields (can be used in nuclear fusions)

Properties of Matter

In gr 11 chem, only concerned with

  • Solids
    • Have definite shape
    • Don’t compress
    • Don’t flow
  • Liquids
    • Assume shape of container
    • Don’t compress
    • Readily flows
  • Gases
    • Fills entire volume of container
    • Highly compressible
    • Readily flows

Kinetic Molecular Theory

  • The idea that all substances contain particles that are in constant motion
  1. Solids
    1. Tend to have vibrational motion
  2. Liquids 2. Tend to have vibrational, rotationtinal, and translational motion
  3. Gases 3. Tend to have mainly translational energy
  • Average kinetic energy of a system = temperature

Pressure

  • Pressure = amount of force per unit area
  • SI unit is the Pascal (Pa): 1Pa = 1N / m^2
Unit Name Conversion
Pascal (Pa) 1 Pa = 1N/m^2
Atmosphere (atm) 1atm = 101.325 kPa (exactly)
Millimeter Mercury (mmHg) 760mmHg = 1atm = 101.325 kPa
Torr (torr) 1 torr = 1 mmHg

Why mmHg?

  • Most measurement devices for measuring pressure is based on manometer
  • Measures distance between base and top of the mercury in mm (hence mmHg)
    • Increase pressure, higher reading
    • Decrease pressure, lower reading

Standard Pressures

  • Atmospheric pressure at sea level (chosen to be standard pressure value for pressure measurements)

  • STP: Standard temperature and pressure (101.325 kPa @ 0 deg C)

    • STP was changed in 1982 to be 100kPa @ 0 deg C

  • SATP: Standard Ambient Temperature and Pressure (100kPa @ 25 deg C)

    • SATP is often used since most labs aren’t operated at 0 deg C

Gas Laws

Boyle’s Law - relationship between pressure and volume

  • As pressure on a gas increases, volume of gas proportionally decreases (when temperature and amount of gas is constant) $$ P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 $$

Charles’s Law - relationship between volume and temperature

  • Volume of a gas varies directly with its temperature (in Kelvin), if the pressure and the amount of gas is constant
  • As temperature increases, the volume proportionally increases $$ \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} $$

Gay-Lussac’s Law - relationshiop between pressure and temperature

  • Pressure exerted by a gas varies directly with absolute temperature if volume and amount of gas (in mole) is constant
  • As temperature increases, pressure proportionally increases $$ \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2} $$

Kelvin & Absolute Zero

  • If gases are cooled, their volume decreases

  • Gases show the same extrapolated point when their volume would be zero

    • The point is known as absolute zero
    • Tidbit: negative kelvins are possible

  • Kelvin scale starts at absolute zero, and 1 degC = 1K

  • 0K = -273.15 deg C

    • To convert b/w C to K, add 273.15
    • To convert b/w K to C, subtract 273.15

  • Gas law calculations are done in Kelvin

Combined Gas Law

Since

  • Boyle’s law PV = a constant value (T and n are constant)
  • Charle’s law V/T = a constant value (P and n are constant)
  • Gay-Lussac’s law P/T = a constant value (V and n are constant)

A combined gas law can be made and be used in all cases

$$ \frac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2V_2}{T_2} $$

Ideal Gas Law

  • All gases in gr 11 chem are treated using a hypothetical state known as Ideal Gas
  • Ideal Gas: a hypothetical gas composed of particles that have zero size, travel in straight lines and have no attraction to each other (no intermolecular forces)
    • Most gases behave similarly to an ideal gas, which makes the ideal gas law viable for real world approximation

$$ \because V \propto \frac{1}{P}_ {\text{Boyle’s Law}} \text{ and } V \propto T_{\text{Charle’s Law}} \text{ and } P \propto T_{\text{Gay-Lussac’s Law}} \text{ and } V \propto n $$

$$ \therefore \text{They are all proportional and related to one another, by a gas constant } R $$

$$ R = 8.314 \text{ kPa} \times \frac{\text{L}}{\text{mol} \times \text{K}} $$ $$ R = 0.08206 \text{ atm} \times \frac{\text{L}}{\text{mol} \times \text{K}} $$

Ideal Gas Law

$$ PV = nRT $$

  • Now moles can be included in calculations
    • 1 mole of ideal gas at STP has a volume of 22.4L
